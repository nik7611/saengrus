God of the Young and the Restless
      In our minds God is old. Beyond old, he is ancient. While we may mentally affirm that God exists beyond time, we still link him with well…being old. Michelangelo’s masterpiece “The Creation of Adam” in the Cistene Chapel doesn’t help as he painted God with flowing white hair and beard.

       We may think that God prefers older people because they have gotten over things like silliness and first crushes and education and career decisions and racing hormones and loudness and pent-up energy and experimentation and idealism, thinking they can be anything, thinking they can change the world. Older people may be more to God’s liking. Old people have time to be religious. Your golden years are the perfect time to draw near to God. There are some notable seniors in the Bible like Abraham and Sarah who at 100 and 90 years old respectively had a miracle baby and Moses, who at 80 led the people of Israel out of Egyptian slavery. But the Bible shouts out loud that God is not shy or slow about working through younger people and even young people.

       Today we are going to look at a young Jewish girl, one of a number of famous young girls in the Bible. Her name is Haddaseh, but if you are familiar with the book of Esther in the Older Testament, you probably know her as Esther.
She grew up in what is now Iran near the Iran-Iraq border just north of the Persian Gulf in Susa which was the capital of the Persian Empire in the fifth century B.C. A century earlier, her people had been brought there by the Babylonian king Nebuchadnezer. The Babylonians had fallen to the Persians and now a Persian king named Xerxes was the absolute master of his empire. In his empire men were in charge and he was the man among men. On one occasion, his queen Vashti refused to follow a direct command. Wanting to be seen as the ruler of his own house, he banned her forever from his presence.

       Let’s pick up the story in
Esther 2:1-11.
       “But after Xerxes’ anger had subsided, he began thinking about Vashti and what she had done and the decree he had made. So his personal attendants suggested, ‘Let us search the empire to find beautiful young virgins for the king. Let the king appoint agents in each province to bring these beautiful young women into the royal harem at the fortress of Susa. Hegai, the king’s eunuch in charge of the harem, will see that they are all given beauty treatments. After that, the young woman who most pleases the king will be made queen instead of Vashti.’ This advice was very appealing to the king, so he put the plan into effect. At that time there was a Jewish man in the fortress of Susa whose name was Mordecai son of Jair. He was from the tribe of Benjamin and was a descendant of Kish and Shimei. His family had been among those who, with King Jehoiachin of Judah, had been exiled from Jerusalem to Babylon by King Nebuchadnezzar. This man had a very beautiful and lovely young cousin, Hadassah, who was also called Esther. When her father and mother died, Mordecai adopted her into his family and raised her as his own daughter.

       As a result of the king’s decree, Esther, along with many other young women, was brought to the king’s harem at the fortress of Susa and placed in Hegai’s care. Hegai was very impressed with Esther and treated her kindly. He quickly ordered a special menu for her and provided her with beauty treatments. He also assigned her seven maids specially chosen from the king’s palace, and he moved her and her maids into the best place in the harem.

       Esther had not told anyone of her nationality and family background, because Mordecai had directed her not to do so. Every day Mordecai would take a walk near the courtyard of the harem to find out about Esther and what was happening to her.”
This story sounds like the reality TV show “Bachelor.” A beautiful girl rises from anonymity to superstardom from a huge group of hopefuls and one man gets to pick the winner. But for all the publicity, Ether is in hiding. No one knows she is a Jewess. Now some Jews like Mordecai had risen to prominence in the Persian Empire, but common sense dictated that you kept quiet about being a Jew. Not everyone would be pleased to have Jews gaining power. So Esther hid it.

       Hiding is the original game. We play peek-a-boo with babies. We play hide and seek when we were kids that ranged over the whole house and yard or even the entire playground, hiding has an appeal. It gives a sense of controlling what is known about us and when it is revealed. We hide many things about ourselves that we do not want others to know. There is a time to hide.

       We hide our first names in the telephone book and leave an initial. We hide our personal information like passwords and PIN numbers. We hide keys and other items in certain places for safety or security reasons. At times we hide our opinions and thoughts and reactions. Sometimes the best face to put on is a poker face! We try to be wise in our relationships as we slowly reveal more and more of who we are to the other person. Sometimes we know that there are things we will keep hidden from the other person because it is not appropriate for that relationship. Sometimes we may find ourselves physically hiding from people. There is a time to hide.
But there is a time to show your true self, to not hold back, to be vulnerable. As it says in Ecclesiastes 3, there is a time to be silent and a time to speak.

       Esther was silent all the way through what amounted to a beauty pageant. All the way through she was admired by all the officials and eventually won the king’s heart. He made her the queen. She still kept her Jewish identity a secret.

       Mordecai continued loyally in Xerses’s civil service, once exposing a plot to kill the king. Everything seemed to be going along well until Mordecai angered a high-ranking Persian noble named Haman. Haman planned to kill Mordecai, but when he learned that he was a Jew, a people whom his ancestors had hated, he thought “Better yet, I will kill all of the Jews in the entire empire.” He told the king “There’s a people living in Persia who don’t obey your laws and should not be tolerated. I’ll get rid of them for you at my own expense.” There would be plenty of people throughout the empire eager to help. “Do with them as you please.” When the word got out about the impending massacre, all the Jews in the empire, including Mordecai, were beside themselves with grief. Esther heard of his distress & sent a servant to find out what was wrong. She was hidden away in blissful ignorance in the king’s harem and hadn’t heard the news.

       Mordecai sent word back about the planned extermination and said she had to go before the king to plead for her people. “I can’t do that. You know what happens to anyone who approaches the king in the inner court without being summoned. They’re as good as dead, unless he holds out his gold scepter and spares them. And besides it’s been 30 days since he called for me.”

       Mordecai’s answer came back. It was strong. It was blunt. It was decisive. “Don't think that just because you are the queen and live in the king's house you're the one Jew who will get out of this alive. If you persist in staying silent at a time like this, help and deliverance will arrive for the Jews from someplace else; but you and your family will be wiped out.” Mordecai appears to believe that a protecting Presence among the Jews (the word “God” does not appear anywhere in the book) would not allow them to be wiped out and would deal with those who let it happen. Then he lightens his tone and wistfully adds “Who knows? Maybe you were made queen for just such a time as this.” He was calling her to act, to out herself to the king. To come out of hiding. There is a time to be silent and a time to speak. A time to hide and a time to come out of hiding.

       Esther replied “You better get everyone to fast for me for three days and then I’ll go to the king, even though it's forbidden. If I die, I die.”

       What happens after those 3 days is nothing short of amazing. Esther went to the king. She was spared. Esther pleaded with him to spare her people from the man plotting their destruction. Haman was exposed and executed for his treachery. Mordecai was promoted to the highest position in the land next to the king for his loyalty. The Jews were saved and their enemies were destroyed. And Jews the world over celebrate this rescue every year as the Feast of Purim (March 3 this year). All this because a young woman came out of hiding when it was time.
Sometimes it is time to hide. People in the Bible had times when hiding was the smart thing to do. David hid in wilderness caves when his enemies were out to kill him. Hills were a good place to hide. But David knew that the mountains provided little protection. He needed a better hiding place. He wrote Psalm 121 1-2 I look up to the mountains; does my help come from mountains? No, my help comes from God, who made heaven, and earth, and mountains…He is your Guardian God, right at your side to protect you. Psalm 32:7 You are my hiding place; you will protect me from trouble and surround me with songs of deliverance.

       Psalm 143:9 Rescue me from my enemies, O LORD, for I hide myself in you. Jesus hid on occasion. John 8:59 - Once when Jesus angered his opponents with something he said, they picked up stones to stone him, but Jesus hid himself, slipping away from the temple grounds. It was not his time. Right then it was a time to hide. Maybe you need to hide right now. God is not against hiding. He offers himself as a hiding place. A place to be quiet and alone and protected. Is it time for you to hide? Or is God calling you out into a new space, to do a new thing, to engage a new sense of vocation? To take on an assignment? To exercise a fledgling spiritual gift?

       There is a time to hide and a time to come out of hiding. Both are actions of trust. “Retreat!” is as wise as “Charge!” What time is it for you? Where are you in some danger and need to hide believing in God protection? Where do you need to come out of hiding in surrender to God’s call?


       Dave Bielby
