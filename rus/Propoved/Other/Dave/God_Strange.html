God's Strange Sense of Timing
In a few days cries of "Happy Holidays" will fill the air. For those who want to be more pointed and I am among them, we will say "Merry Christmas." But if we want to go further in the spirit of the actual nativity story as it is written in the Bible, I am suggesting that we adopt a new greeting this year. Let's wish each other "A Very Inconvenient Christmas and a Truly Interrupted New Year!" After all, that's how it all began. We love convenience and we don't like interruptions but this is precisely how Jesus, the Son of God, came to earth, with great inconvenience and as a huge interruption.
      
       However we are preparing for Christmas this year, all of us are invited to hear how the Son of God became human in one Mary of Nazareth, an unsuspecting young girl in her teens who probably wanted her life to go as smoothly as you hope yours will go, but who got royally interrupted by God and had to deal with it as best she knew how. Luke's gospel tells the story of what has come to be known as the Annunciation or the Announcement. Read Luke 1:26-45
The Birth of Jesus Foretold
       26In the sixth month, God sent the angel Gabriel to Nazareth, a town in Galilee,
       27to a virgin pledged to be married to a man named Joseph, a descendant of David. The virgin's name was Mary.
       28The angel went to her and said, "Greetings, you who are highly favored! The Lord is with you."
       29Mary was greatly troubled at his words and wondered what kind of greeting this might be.
       30But the angel said to her, "Do not be afraid, Mary, you have found favor with God.
       31You will be with child and give birth to a son, and you are to give him the name Jesus.
       32He will be great and will be called the Son of the Most High. The Lord God will give him the throne of his father David,
       33and he will reign over the house of Jacob forever; his kingdom will never end."
       34"How will this be," Mary asked the angel, "since I am a virgin?"
       35The angel answered, "The Holy Spirit will come upon you, and the power of the Most High will overshadow you. So the holy one to be born will be called[c] the Son of God.
       36Even Elizabeth your relative is going to have a child in her old age, and she who was said to be barren is in her sixth month.
       37For nothing is impossible with God."
       38"I am the Lord's servant," Mary answered. "May it be to me as you have said." Then the angel left her.

Mary Visits Elizabeth
       39At that time Mary got ready and hurried to a town in the hill country of Judea,
       40where she entered Zechariah's home and greeted Elizabeth.
       41When Elizabeth heard Mary's greeting, the baby leaped in her womb, and Elizabeth was filled with the Holy Spirit.
       42In a loud voice she exclaimed: "Blessed are you among women, and blessed is the child you will bear!
       43But why am I so favored, that the mother of my Lord should come to me?
       44As soon as the sound of your greeting reached my ears, the baby in my womb leaped for joy.
       45Blessed is she who has believed that what the Lord has said to her will be accomplished!"
Mary was living a normal life for a young girl and she was pledged or betrothed to be married as were girls her age. She was set for life. And then Gabriel the angel appeared. Do you ever notice: in the Bible angels got into the very bad habit of never knocking? Good morning, favourite girl. Who, me? The Lord is with you. He is?! Gabriel, not known for small talk continues.
      
       He doesn't ask for her cooperation. There is no "Please", no "Would you ever consider being the mother of the Son of the Most High?" This is not a proposal, a request. God doesn't observe any of the language of partnership and signs of collaboration and working together that we expect from people. This angel is pretty aggressive. Some of us may feel ambivalent about this: How could he just drop this into the life of a young girl?! Would I like my own daughter put in this predicament? Can young Mary even be held responsible for her part in this conversation? It is hardly a level playing field, a young girl versus a seasoned angel.
      
       Did Gabriel assume it was good news any way you looked at it? He may have had no idea of the impact of his words. He just says "You're going to have a baby. That's the plan." What would an angel know about having a baby anyway?! Can he appreciate what this news will mean to Mary?! She might have preferred an ordinary day, a peaceful period of betrothal, followed by marriage to Joseph and an ordinary family and a nice life. That was taken away by Gabriel. Was Mary really blessed or just really interrupted?
      
       Angels are fine as long as they are hanging glittering and silent in your Christmas tree or window or perched woodenly on a knickknack shelf. The real ones are a little more troublesome.
      
       How is she supposed to respond? Maybe her eyes catch sight of a book on her night-table Personal Boundaries: Regaining Control of your Life. It was a good read, but she couldn't remember anything in it about angels. Maybe she wonders about negotiating for more time. Uh, Mr. Angel, I'd like to think about this. In fact, I'd like to fast and pray about this. I'd like to get my youth group down at the synagogue to pray as well. And I'd like to talk to my parents about this. You know that honour your father and mother thing in the law. Better tell them before than after don't you think?! Dad. Mom. I'm pregnant. This is the kind of thing you'd like your daughter to talk with you about first right? But as if to deny all these reasonable requests, Gabriel has already spoken: "You will be with child and you will give birth to a son and you are to give him the name Jesus. That's the plan." This is one bossy angel. Some people say God is a gentleman. If his angels are any indication, I wonder about that.
      
       We don't like teachers who pile on homework like theirs is the only class we take and we have no life other than to do their assignments. We bristle against demanding family members who act like their lives are more important than ours, their "Yes" more reasonable than our "No." We don't like people who think they can waltz into our life any time they want and expect us to drop everything and do what they want. We can find ourselves resisting them even if their name is Gabriel. Even if it is God. We call these people forward, presumptuous, brazen, bold, nervy, difficult. We may be shocked to realize that God is somewhat like these people that we resist. Paul Scott Wilson says it well, "We encounter God as total acceptance and total demand."
      
       Living with God is all about entertaining these troublesome and unpredictable angels, if I can continue the imagery, who show up at inconvenient times and places. Sometimes these annunciations don't seem like good news. They may look like blows or set-backs, interruptions and intrusions into our well-planned lives. Occasionally the news is good, but annunciations can take the form of downright bad news: illness, rejection, loss, failure and bereavement. And we might get angry.
      
       Jeanette Bakke writes "Most of our lives are spent somewhere between flight from God and complete agreement with God, between being keenly aware of or oblivious to God. Our responses are mixed. We sense the Spirit asking us to release our whole selves into God's care and our answer is a conflicted 'Yes & No'. Even though we would like to be God's without reservation, we also like having a sense of control over our destiny. Releasing our life to another, even to God, is not easy."
      
       It wasn't easy for Mary. She was confused. "How, I am a virgin?" Other miraculous births had always involved a man and a woman, perhaps old, perhaps incapable of having children, but never just one woman on her own! Somehow Gabriel must have communicated to her that this was soon, not five years down the road. Was Joseph going to show up tonight and say "Let's elope. We're going to Vegas." Would she be forced against her will by someone? Would she break her pledge to Joseph?
      
       Maybe Gabriel sits down here. For all his gruffness, he's not all business. He wears God's heart on his angelic sleeve. While God is intrusive, he does not run roughshod over Mary. While he is Lord he is not belligerent. While sovereign, he is not brutal. He is love itself.
      
       Gabriel explains this conception. He doesn't get overly technical, which may be a disappointment for our DNA-curious culture. This will not turn into the first episode of CSI Nazareth, Conception Scene Investigation. It is about divine presence. He says "The Spirit of God will work a miracle in your womb. No human father will be involved, only the overshadowing power and presence of God, the Spirit of life, so your son will be God's Son, the Lord. God can do the impossible"
      
       Then he adds Oh Mary, you know your old, childless aunt Elizabeth? By God's grace, she's expecting. She's six months along and understands something about miraculous births, not exactly like yours, but close enough.
      
       This is Mary's moment. The ball is in her court. What will she do? She does a remarkable thing. I can see her in my mind's eye, drawing herself up to her full height, squaring her shoulders in a way that would make any dad or mom proud, and looking straight into the eyes of the angel. Mr. Gabriel, you are looking at the Lord's servant. This is willing submission, a strong surrender with no hint of a begrudging spirit.
      
       Then she expresses her heart in the form of a prayer - may it happen to me just as you say. God's call becomes Mary's prayer. The idea of being the Lord's servant wasn't something that came into her head during this conversation. Doesn't pull it out for special religious occasions when the music moves her. This is not sentimental language. She speaks it when her whole life is in the balance. It was how she saw herself. To use Paul's words in Romans 12, Mary was presenting her body as a living sacrifice to God. Perhaps to Mary, God was not the least bit intrusive? She was his servant, always at his beck & call.

       Maybe we're jealous of Mary? "I'd say Yes to God if I knew everything like Mary did. Gabriel laid it all out, plain and clear. Really? Did Mary know all that was involved? Was it a fully informed "Yes"? No "Yes" is ever fully informed. We always have unanswered questions. There is always mystery in saying "Yes" to God whether you're saying "Yes" for the very first time in your life or saying "Yes" for the hundredth time.

       So where can we find the ability to say a willing yes? God gave Mary a willing & courageous heart. There is a verse in Phil 2 that shows what is happening in Mary's heart. It is what happens in our hearts too. Phil 2:13 - God is working in you, giving you the desire and the power to do what pleases him. We can trust his work in us and we will be able to do what is inconvenient, what is unreasonable. Things we never thought we would be able to do. We can forgive. Give. Conquer. Serve. Love. Go there. Do this. Be reconciled to each other. We can believe. We can carry our cross. We can go the distance. We can do whatever he calls us to do.

       Can we take a "pass"? Will God take a "No"? Mary could have said "No." Saying "No" is a means of birth control. "God, I'd like to abstain please." Saying "No" to God is always an option. How will God respond? Who knows? How will your own soul live after saying "No"? Who knows? All we do know for certain is that "No" is not the way of love and trust. Neither is it the way of courage and faith. And it was not Mary's way. Some call it surrendering to love. You may simply call it saying a strong "Yes" to God.

       Mary is a model to all of us about how to live with and for God. She is our older spiritual sister. Later in Luke 1 she composed a praise song about how God looked into a world full of big powerful people and chose an easily overlooked girl for special glory. Mary feels more blessed than Queen Elizabeth, Martina Navratilova, Belinda Stronach and Oprah Winfrey all rolled into one!

       Pregnancy and childbirth involve discomfort and pain. People who have decided to live life with God instead of against him do not avoid obedience because it is hard and because it might involve pain. Eugene Peterson prayed "God, I don't want to go through my life always looking for easy, painless paths. I want to find the true way, and the right path." Gabriel has appeared. God is at work interrupting you, calling you to do the inconvenient and painful. But the inconvenience and pain of obedience leads to joy.

       I remember when we were expecting our first child Heather. We were in the labour room. Most of the pain and inconvenience was Margie's. Okay, all of the pain! I was keeping track of the length of contractions and the space between them on a piece of paper on the other side of the room. I would dutifully leave Marg during her contractions to record the info, when she finally growled "Bring the paper over here!" I think I remember there were a few other words she said but I can't quite recall them right now. And then finally after all the pain the joy of our first child!

       Mary knew this joy even though everything about being pregnant and giving birth was not enjoyable. Brian Stiller, the president of Tyndale Seminary wrote "I've been asked-especially since I've moved into academic leadership-'Brian, are you enjoying your work?' My only honest response is, 'The question I wish you'd ask is, "Are you doing what you should be doing?" To that I answer, 'Yes.'" Obedience becomes the operative word and obedience brings its own particular deep joy.

       So in the spirit of wanting you all to have a life of joy with God, I wish you all "A Very Inconvenient Christmas and a Truly Interrupted New Year!"


       Dave Bielby
10 December 2006
