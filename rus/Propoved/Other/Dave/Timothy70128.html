Timothy: What the Young and the Restless Need
      I'm not what you would call a daredevil. I don't bungee jump or sky-dive. I like to do new things and I don't mind doing some things that have an element of risk to them but I don't tend to push the limits too hard. There are some people who love to do that. They are up for anything intensely physical, anything extreme or even dangerous. But not me.

       Most of the challenges that I face test me in ways other than physical endurance or psychological toughness. But they are demanding because they deal with people.
Difficult conversations.
Complex situations.
Massive egos.
Brittle egos.
Uncontrolled reactions.
Deep brokenness.
Spiritual darkness.
The struggle to believe.
Crushing disappointment.
Fierce resistance.
Diverse personalities.
Rampant confusion.

When doing the right thing will cost dearly. And frankly, it takes more from me than I have to give.

       It is those kinds of things that sometimes are too much for you as well right? And when there is a lot of it going on around you, you feel like you are in the deep end of the pool-in way over your head.
      Timothy felt like he was in over his head. And being timid by nature, as he seemed to be, didn't make it any easier! Timothy was the apostle Paul's young associate. He had been hand picked by Paul on one of his missionary journeys through Timothy's hometown. The believers there couldn't say enough good things about Timothy and so Paul decided to take him with him. Timothy traveled around with Paul a few years gaining experience and growing up in his faith. At one point, they were in Ephesus working with the church there. It was experiencing some problems with people who were spreading destructive ideas. Paul had to leave and so told Timothy to stay there and quote "stop them."

       Over the course of time that Timothy was there Paul wrote him two letters, 1st and 2nd Timothy to give him guidance and to encourage him in this tough assignment.

       Here's a bit from the second letter. 1:3-7: Timothy, I thank God for you-the God I serve with a clear conscience, just as my ancestors did. Night and day I constantly remember you in my prayers. I long to see you again, for I remember your tears as we parted. And I will be filled with joy when we are together again. I remember your genuine faith, for you share the faith that first filled your grandmother Lois and your mother, Eunice. And I know that same faith continues strong in you. This is why I remind you to fan into flames the spiritual gift God gave you when I laid my hands on you. For God has not given us a spirit of fear and timidity, but of power, love, and self-discipline.

       With these words Paul encourages young Timothy is his task which was to stop the people who were spreading ideas that opposed what Paul had taught. Now there may be some people who respond to this by saying "Here's another classic case of the church bullying people and trying to shut down opinions that they don't like. Another instance of the powerbrokers of the church trying to shut up people who want to explore other ideas. That's how it works according to the Da Vinci code conspiracy. Stomp out ideas and alternatives and options that go against what you want people to believe. Church is all about controlling and manipulating people, controlling what you think, directing what you believe. Why not just love each other and leave each other alone?

       Need to consider that Paul was trying to protect the church from ideas not simply because they were different or new but because of what they produced in people. The gospel of God's love in Jesus produced people of faith and love. Paul wanted the church to be a place where loved reigned supreme and where people would live with interior purity and freedom and strong faith. These new teachings would destroy faith and love and enslave people and that's why Paul was against them.

       Listen: "The purpose of my instruction is that all believers would be filled with love that comes from a pure heart, a clear conscience, and genuine faith. But some people have missed this whole point. They have turned away from these things, trying to make a name for themselves." Paul wasn't down on free thinkers per se, but he was against destructive teaching that didn't help people live out the life of faith in God in love.
      Timothy felt intimidated by this task. He lacked courage. Maybe he was tempted to be nice as opposed to helpful. Maybe he hoped the problem would go away on its own. Maybe he wanted to quit. His anxiety could have come from many sources. It was a hard task on its own. There were some powerful individuals to deal with, people who were older than he was. At one point Paul encourages Timothy "Don't let anyone think less of you because you are young."

       His personality? Timothy may not have had a presence that commanded respect. Perhaps his home situation had been influential this way. We are told in Acts that 16 that Timothy came form a mixed home, maybe even a mixed up home. His father was a Greek and his mother was Jewish. His mother Eunice was a believer in Jesus as the Messiah as was her mother Lois, but Timothy's father was not.

       Two parents with two different ways of life and Timothy caught in the middle. How to respect your father and follow the faith of your mother? Did his dad make it tough? Did this potentially difficult home life cause any insecurity in Timothy? It wouldn't be the first time. Brought this weakness into his faith and ministry. We always bring who we are into what we do. Our doing is filled with our being.
Paul is saying "Timothy I want you to focus on the good things that God has given you through your home life, from your mother and grandmother. They have been full of faith in their lives and have given you a great start in life. They taught you the Scriptures from an early age. Their faith enabled them to live well and face all their challenges in life. Faith found a home in your granny's heart and your mother's heart & I am convinced it lives in you now. Timothy, it's your turn now.

       Stir up in your heart the work of the Spirit, the gift of God that you received when I laid hands on you. This would have been some apostolic or pastoral gift, the ability to serve Jesus in a specific way. This verse invites us to use our gifts to participate in what God is doing. Timothy probably had a sense of the gift that he received. Paul may have even spoken about the gift or given a prophecy about Timothy that pointed to the nature of the gift as he laid hands on Timothy. It is not always as clear to us.
          Guess - not wildly, talk, read, pray, listen to your heart (God is at work), explore
           Inquire about opportunities - how could I use this?
           Forge Ahead
           Test the experience - involve others - how did it go?
           Serve
      Paul was saying "Don't be afraid to use your gift Timothy even if it is a difficult role." Then this great statement "For God has not given us a spirit of fear and timidity, but of power, love, and self-discipline." That is a great truth. Courage is a response to God's Spirit within us. The Holy Spirit that God has given to us is not a Spirit that causes us to give in to our insecurities and anxieties. He is not a "You can't do that" Spirit, always rubbing our noses in our limitations to keep us in line. It is his pleasure to make us capable for the task. He is the Spirit of power.

       He enables you to fulfill the tasks that your gifts qualify you to assume & that God calls you to do. It's important to understand that power always has parameters.
We hear today that one of the greatest things that we can say to children is "You can do anything!" We understand the potential that lies within children and we want to encourage that. It certainly seems appropriate to encourage children to dream big. On the other hand, it may not be totally realistic to say "You can do anything!" Anything? I want to create a new star. I want to swim to England.

       Later on, many of us understand that "You can do anything" doesn't always fit with human limitations. Perhaps it would be better if people said "You can do something. Now go and do it!" Maturity & time teach us another layer of wisdom: I will do what I can.

       But there is another path, the way of deepest wisdom that God has always pointed to and led us into: "I will do what the Lord gives me power to do." SAY IT: I will do what the Lord gives me power to do. Paul is saying. Timothy, the Holy Spirit of power gives you courage among people who have only their own power.
Not unqualified power. God's power is always exercised in love. He is the Spirit of love. We tend to think of loving people as gentle and soft. That is to misread love. To romanticize it. Dietrich Bonhoeffer knew about love. Bonhoeffer was a young courageous Lutheran theologian who offered one of the first clear voices of resistance to Adolf Hitler. Bonhoeffer openly challenged his church to stand with the Jews in their time of need. He was a great examples of moral courage in the face of extreme difficulty and danger." This is what he wrote about love.

       "The people who love, because they are freed (from themselves) through the truth of God, are the most revolutionary people on earth. They are the ones who upset all values; they are the explosives in human society. Such persons are the most dangerous." Love for God & people gives us the power to stand against great difficulty & challenge. It is like Paul is saying to Timothy "In this situation of dangerous doctrines, let love make you the dangerous one. People who are not concerned with themselves can be free to be dangerous on behalf of others. People who have been liberated from self-centeredness are dangerous to the small people who are only wrapped up in themselves." The Spirit of love gives courage.
Moves us to do dangerous things. While I would not go after a wild animal for no reason, I would attack it if it were after one of my children. Love breeds courage.

       He is the Spirit of self-discipline. Sound mind - clear disciplined thinking and living. This does not come naturally to younger people. It is something that deepens over time spent living with God. Read Proverbs.

       Paul tells Timothy in another place to "Flee the evil desires of youth." Tis is usually seen as a reference to illicit and destructive sexual activity (Perhaps, but sexual sin is no respecter of ages). I think it may refer to the temptation that young people experience, the temptation to think they know it all or at least know more than older people. I know what you younger people are thinking: You're saying "I knew that." It is the temptation to think you know and to speak and not to listen to others. LISTEN: Flee the evil desires of youth and pursue righteousness, faith, love and peace, along with those who call on the Lord out of a pure heart. Don't have anything to do with foolish and stupid arguments, because you know they produce quarrels. And the Lord's servant must not quarrel; instead, he must be kind to everyone, able to teach, not resentful. Those who oppose him he must gently instruct.

       Power enables us, love frees us, wisdom guides us, gifts of the Spirit in us bring courage.
The good news is this: Timothy need never say "I didn't do it because I was afraid."
Neither do we.


       Dave Bielby
