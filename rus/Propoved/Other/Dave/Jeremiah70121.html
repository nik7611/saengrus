Jeremiah: Doing What You Should Be Doing
      "What are you doing?!" This is the question that parents call out when their children in the next room are unusually quiet.

       "What do you do?" This is the question that comes up in conversation among adults. It wants to find out what is important to us: what people do.

       "Are you doing what you're supposed to be doing?" This is the question that gets to the heart of your life.
Jeremiah 1:
1 The words of Jeremiah son of Hilkiah, one of the priests at Anathoth in the territory of Benjamin.
2 The word of the LORD came to him in the thirteenth year of the reign of Josiah son of Amon king of Judah,
3 and through the reign of Jehoiakim son of Josiah king of Judah, down to the fifth month of the eleventh year of Zedekiah son of Josiah king of Judah, when the people of Jerusalem went into exile. The Call of Jeremiah
4 The word of the LORD came to me, saying,
5 "Before I formed you in the womb I knew [a] you, before you were born I set you apart; I appointed you as a prophet to the nations."
6 "Ah, Sovereign LORD," I said, "I do not know how to speak; I am only a child."
7 But the LORD said to me, "Do not say, 'I am only a child.' You must go to everyone I send you to and say whatever I command you.
8 Do not be afraid of them, for I am with you and will rescue you," declares the LORD.
9 Then the LORD reached out his hand and touched my mouth and said to me, "Now, I have put my words in your mouth.
10 See, today I appoint you over nations and kingdoms to uproot and tear down, to destroy and overthrow, to build and to plant."
Jeremiah was a young man in the morally and socially corrupt kingdom of Judah in 627 B.C. One of his contemporaries was young King Josiah (21 at the time) who was trying to turn the people back to God. But it's like Jeremiah is in the stands watching Josiah fighting it out down on the playing field. We call out "What are you doing Jeremiah?" Oh, I'm watching King Josiah clean up the kingdom of Judah. I'm a big fan of his. See I have my Josiah T-shirt (Number 8 - that's how old he was when he became king you know) & I know all his stats. He's doing a great job isn't he, burning shrines & tearing down altars & purging the temple of all the idols & dealing with all the unfaithful priests. Go Josiah Go!"

       Then somewhere in between Jeremiah's first hot dog and his Big Slurpee, God speaks to him. "I don't just want you to be a fan, I want you on the team. I want you out on the field. I've had my eye on you for a quite a while now and I want you to be my prophet in all of this mess."
Now prophets have always been a mixed bag. The church and even the general culture recognize prophets that comes from time to time, or at least people who somehow have a prophetic presence, those people who have a message that transcends all the noise, a message that reaches into the collective soul of a nation or a church and calls it to be something beyond what it is now. That's the good side. Then there have always been the charlatans, the fakes, those who present themselves as prophets so they can hear the sound of their own voice or help themselves to the money of the gullible or spout the latest and most popular theories on all the hot button topics.

       God is at work within this mixed bag & he raises people up to be his prophets, to speak his words, to announce his intentions, in a culture or situation that needs to hear them. We are always made aware in the Bible that there are true prophets & fake ones and we are told to be careful. What fruit do their lives produce? What character do they exhibit? Jeremiah is a tested and approved prophet in all ways.

       God said to him, "I've had my eye on you for a while now and I want you to be my prophet in all of this mess. God has forethought and intention. Not - Oh great, Hilkiah and his wife just had a baby - now what am I going to do with it? I have a world full of people and they have just made my responsibility one person heavier.

       God is saying to Jeremiah "I knew all about you and chose you before I formed you in your mother's womb, before you ever came to be. Before you were born I purposed for you to be my prophet to my people and to the nations." What an enormous sense of gift comes with these words! That God would have such intimate knowledge of us. He knows us by name.

      God made all the delicate, inner parts of your body and knit you together in your mother's womb. He made you so wonderfully complex! His workmanship is marvelous. He watched you as you were being formed in utter seclusion, as you were woven together in the dark of the womb. He saw you before you were born with an insight far greater than any ultrasound could reveal. Every day of your life was recorded in his book. Every moment was laid out before a single day had passed. Make sure we do not understand this as teaching that everything in your life is planned and you have to live out your fate, but that God is thoroughly acquainted with his loving intentions for you and how he longs to see them unfold. As he was with Jeremiah.

       But Jeremiah was resistant. We have these movements within. You know what I'm talking about, the rationalization, refusal, hiding from God, dismissing the thought, filling the space with noise and activity, somehow excusing ourselves and trying to get around what God is saying to us.

       Refusal never sounds so right as when it is reverent. Listen to Jeremiah: O Sovereign LORD, there's no way. We can do this: "Highest and true God, creator of all the universe & saviour of my soul through Jesus Christ your son, no."

       I'm too young. Only a youth unqualified by training and experience. Sprinkle a little self-critical remark on top like (Your work is too holy or important for me to be involved) and it can feel like a trump card. Be apologetic Ah, I sorry about this Lord, but…I really wish I could, but… "Can't" is often a way of saying "won't".

       God is pretty abrupt here - Stop that kind of talk. (God knows that if we say something often enough we may begin to believe it!) You're going to do this for me. God addresses Jeremiah's fear of people. If God were going to send us out under our own steam then yes it is an issue! BUT…I will be with you. I will protect you. I, the LORD, have spoken. As if to say this conversation is over, but then he goes on. Look what he does. It is not what he says but what he does next.

       Reached out to Jeremiah and touched his mouth. Now you have my words in your mouth. Now sometimes we don't like it when people put words in our mouth. "Let me speak for myself!" On the other hand, we don't like trusting people to speak for us. We are picky about who we let speak for us. God isn't so choosy. Today, I appoint you to stand up against nations and kingdoms with a word that will be both destructive and constructive. Serious warnings. We read in 2 Chr. 36:15 that God repeatedly sent his prophets to warn them, for he had compassion on his people.

       This reads like a brief conversation. God may bring us through an extended process - my processes tend to take months. This one seems to take but a moment, one conversation and the issue is over. Not quite. There are some remaining issues that surface later and need to be worked through with God.

       Jeremiah struggled with his calling. Sometimes it was hard for him. Jer. 20:7 O LORD, you misled me, and I allowed myself to be misled. You are stronger than I am, and you overpowered me. Now I am mocked every day; everyone laughs at me. When I speak, the words burst out. "Violence and destruction!" I shout. So these messages from the LORD have made me a household joke. But if I say I'll never mention the LORD or speak in his name, his word burns in my heart like a fire. It's like a fire in my bones! I am worn out trying to hold it in! I can't do it! Just because God said that he would be with him and protect him, didn't mean the Jeremiah had an easy time of it. It did mean and in all of his troubles, God was with him and did protect him even thought he did suffer, his life was kept and God was faithful and trustworthy and dependable.

       So God had intentions for Jeremiah from before his conception. What are God's intentions for you? What are you doing? What are you supposed to be doing? Are you doing what you're supposed to be doing? This is a question that is clarified over time.

       Douglas Shurrman - A modern assumption is that to have a calling is to experience self-fulfillment in that calling.

       Self-fulfillment has become a way of trying to know God's will for our lives. We assume that God's will must always further our sense of self-fulfilment. If I feel fulfilled, I must be in God's will. If I don't I must have missed it. It's not that our Father isn't interested in our sense of fulfillment, or that being fulfilled or wanting to be fulfilled is not appropriate. But it is not central

       Brian Stiller: I've been asked -especially since I've moved into academic leadership-"Brian, are you enjoying your work?" My only honest response is "The question I wish you'd ask is 'Are you doing what you should be doing?' To that I answer 'Yes.'"

       He goes on to say "Self-fulfillment has little to do with the big question "Am I in the place ordered by the Lord?" [Obedience is the word and, in the grace of God, it brings it's own reward.]

       There are moments when we feel rewarded, but that is not the gauge we should use. Good times come momentarily and are gone. Some encounters leave me with self-doubt. Self fulfillment may be a by-product of God's leading. But to make it the test of being in God's will is to distort what obedience is.

       Isaiah 6 - sending you to preach but you won't make a difference. Not too rewarding. Not too fulfilling.

       Closes with this prayer
"Dear Father, I'd trade feelings of self-fulfillment any day for knowing that in some modest way, my life is geared to fulfill your calling."
Amen.

       We can take on more responsibility in this than we need to. God is responsible to call you. We are not a self-called people.
       We are responsible to listen.
       We are responsible to do.
      
       May God equip you with everything good for doing his will, and may he work in us what is pleasing to him, through Jesus Christ, to whom be glory for ever and ever.
Amen.
