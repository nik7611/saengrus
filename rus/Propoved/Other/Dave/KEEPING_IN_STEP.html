KEEPING IN STEP WITH THE SPIRIT
1 Thessalonians 5:19-22
      Last Sunday night as I was turning over in my mind what I had planned to say this morning, I sensed that it had become flat, with no sense of inner life and urgency. I sensed I was being drawn by God to speak on the Spirit and how to respond to his presence among us. This did not surprise me as some questions had been raised during the Father's Heart Conference last weekend about a few things that were witnessed. Keeping in step with the Spirit is also a theme in the spiritual formation of a congregation and so it also fits our present series How God grows People. I will look to the apostle Paul for my guidance.

       Paul is undoubtedly a man of the Spirit - a charismatic who would also say that every believer is a charismatic (charism means grace), that every believer is graced by the Spirit. Paul is not afraid of the Spirit so what he says is not out of fear but wisdom. Paul also knows his way around the world of the Spirit and is a capable and wise guide for us whenever we encounter the Spirit's presence and work. Paul provides guidance for these encounters in a way that bows to the Spirit but also respects the personhood and integrity of every believer. Because he is a man of the Spirit, he is concerned that spiritual ministry be helpful to the spiritual progress of others.

       Before we get to Paul's counsel, let me give a brief history of the Spirit and the church in the Bible. The Spirit has had a checkered, inconsistent experience with the church. Some times were good, others weren't.

       When the Spirit came, he came as the prophet Joel said he would - he came on all flesh, women and men, old and young, servants and free people and now prophecies, visions and dreams were not the ministry of the few but the many. The Spirit had come on everyone and they were all now living in the Spirit. But to quote Paul Since we live by the Spirit, let us keep in step with the Spirit.. We haven't always done that well. The joy-filled days of Pentecost had barely subsided when the Holy Spirit was lied to by a sneaky couple named Ananias and Sapphira who wanted to look good by giving a sizable gift to the church, all they got from the sale of some land so they said, when they kept back some of it. The Spirit was not taken in by the lie, for he cannot be fooled, and for their brazen testing of the Spirit they paid with their lives. Too bad. They could have simply given part of what they got from the sale without lying & it would have been fine.

       People full of the Holy Spirit and wisdom were trusted to care for the vulnerable in the church. The Spirit empowered and guided the early church in prayer and expanding witness. Philip, one of the 12 apostles, entered Samaria and many believed the gospel. One believer named Simon, who had been a headliner in town for quite a while as a sorcerer and had amazed the people with his magic, followed Philip around and saw the signs and miracles he did.

       When Peter and John, and especially Peter who was so instrumental in the Spirit coming on the Jewish believers at Pentecost, came to Samaria and laid their hands on the Samaritan believers, they received the Holy Spirit. When Simon saw that the Spirit could be given by the apostles laying their hands on people, he wanted in on the attention. He offered them money for this power. He was trying to use the power of God to gain a following. Peter responded with "May your money perish with you!"

       Apostles Barnabas and Paul worked in the Spirit's strength. Among the early prophets, one called Agabus and the four daughters of Philip were empowered by the Spirit and served the church well with their words.

       Paul founded a church in the city of Corinth, a church that had received the gifts of the Spirit in full. But they had become riddled with arrogance and selfishness. They had fixated on one expression of the life of the Spirit, speaking in tongues, when he had provided a wide array of gifts. Paul encourages them to broaden their appetite for other gifts, especially prophecy. Paul has to correct them against being discourteous or uncontrolled or disorderly because gifts were all given for the common good. The church had fallen into the sad state of exercising the gifts of the Spirit without love, the primary fruit of the Spirit, and so became of no benefit to anyone - it was just a noisy church.

       People were being filled with the Spirit to boldly share the gospel and to bring hope and compassion and healing. Paul founded a church in Thessalonica. They too needed some help in their life in the Holy Spirit:
1 Thessalonians 5:19-22
Do not put out the Spirit's fire; do not treat prophecies with contempt. Test everything. Hold on to the good. Avoid every kind of evil.
      The Spirit's presence was manifested in part through prophetic words from people who had been given the gift of speaking a word from the Lord. It appears that people were not responsive to these prophecies. They were treating them lightly and in doing so, were unconsciously suppressing the Spirit as he moved within the church. There is to be an openness to the Spirit by being receptive to his words given through gifted people. But Paul doesn't encourage a mindless approach. Paul guides them to be discerning instead of dismissive when it came to prophetic words. Test everything. Hold on to the good. Avoid every kind of evil. Sift and separate the nourishing wheat from the useless chaff. You'll find them both.

       Exercising the prophetic gift was not to be a closed circle. A prophet could not say "The Spirit gave me this gift and I speak what I have been given to speak and you have no part in this." Each person's gift as well as its use belongs not to them, but to the community. And so the community is expected to participate in the prophetic word in two ways: receive it fully and weigh it carefully. Test it. Does it reflect the work of the Spirit or not?

       I have experienced this testing in a fashion in my preaching ministry. At the church I served before, I met with two mature and wise people who would go over my recent messages and give their judgement as to where it lacked the full light and truth of God or whether there was something I was doing that was unhelpful or getting in the way. Painful? Yes! Helpful? Absolutely!

       Perhaps an idea that comes from this passage is that when a church has a speaker in for some meetings, the invitation could include "After your ministry we will be glad to provide you with feedback for the strengthening of your ministry." If they are not agreeable to that I would encourage you to not invite them. That is not quenching the Spirit, but testing everything. Paul's guidance is very even-handed for he is a conscientious charismatic. There is always something to heed, something to watch for regardless of where you find yourself on the charismatic spectrum.

       This brief history shows that the Spirit's history with the church is checkered. So it is in the present. What about when strange things that happen and people attribute it to the Spirit? When you read the whole Bible it is difficult to escape the fact that the Spirit sometimes moved people to do some strange things. How do you test those? Our level of comfort? What makes sense to our 21st century rationalistic Western minds?

       In 1 Sam. 19 the Spirit of God came upon King Saul, and he began to prophesy! He tore off his clothes and lay naked on the ground all day and all night, prophesying in the presence of Samuel. Two prayers arise in me when I think of this "Please Lord, don't ever do that here! If you do, please don't use me! There are plenty of other people you could use!" In Isaiah 20, God told Isaiah "Go, take off your clothes and sandals," and Isaiah did it, going about naked and barefooted.

       In Ezekiel 4, God told Ezekiel to build a model of an army attacking Jerusalem, then get an iron skillet and place it upright between him and the model city. "Lie on your left side for 390 days. Then, turn over and lie down on your right side for 40 days. Look straight at the siege of Jerusalem. Roll up your sleeve, shake your bare arm, and preach against her…I will tie you up with ropes, tie you so you can't move or turn over until you have finished the days of the siege."

       Then he arranged for food. "Bake it out in the open where everyone can see you, using dried human dung for fuel." Now that got to Ezekiel. Lord, I've never contaminated myself with food like that." "All right," God said. "I'll let you bake your bread over cow dung instead of human dung." That doesn't seem much better to me! Enough said. Not everything is like that and that is certainly not the usual but it's there.

       Are these strange manifestations to be attributed to the Spirit? Yes. Are all strange things to be attributed to the Spirit? No. Do we throw it all away? No. Richard Foster encourages us when he says "Rest easy with your fears that some aspect of what you are doing is in the flesh. I can guarantee you there will be flesh in it! To be sure we never want to manipulate others or be manipulated by others, but we should not be afraid to step out and exercise our gifts." Let's allow that freedom to each other. Also let's be teachable and open to testing. Let testing be done in a gracious manner.

       How could we test a prophecy or some manifestation? Some people have found the following to be helpful: Reflection in Scripture (what does the Bible reveal?) Tradition (what does 2000 years of church history show?) Reason (what confirmation comes from careful thinking?) and Experience (what effects are brought to life in our personal and communal journey in Christ?)

       Paul is also aware of the impact of the "strange" on people. In 1 Cor. 14 he comments on how an orderly manifestation of the Spirit will help any inquirers and unbelievers who see it, not to mention brothers and sisters in Christ, as opposed to driving them away and giving them good cause to mock the church.

       Finally, we are told to not grieve the Holy Spirit in our relationships with each other for it is devilish that the one who is keeping us together in his unity would be used to drive us apart.
Ephesians 4:30-32
And do not bring sorrow to God's Holy Spirit by the way you live. Remember, he has identified you as his own, guaranteeing that you will be saved on the day of redemption. Get rid of all bitterness, rage, anger, harsh words, and slander, as well as all types of evil behavior. Instead, be kind to each other, tenderhearted, forgiving one another, just as God through Christ has forgiven you.
I close with this old Hasidic tale: A rabbi asked his students:
       "How can we determine the hour of dawn, when the night ends and the day begins?
One of the rabbi's students suggested:
      "When from a distance you can distinguish between a dog and a sheep?"
       "No," was the answer of the rabbi.
       "Is it when one can distinguish between a fig tree and a grapevine?" asked a second student.
       "No," the rabbi said.
      "Please tell us the answer then," said the students.
       "It is, then," said the wise teacher, "when you can look into the face of another human being and you have enough light in you to recognize your brother or your sister. Until then it is night, and the darkness is still with us."
So, when we look at the people all across the charismatic spectrum and recognize them and treat them as brother and sister, then the darkness is gone and the light has come.

In the name of the Father and the Son and the Holy Spirit.
Amen.

       Dave Bielby
29 October 2006
